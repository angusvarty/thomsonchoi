Heliocentric Parallax

  • This is a method of determining the distance to relatively nearby stars, by observing them relative to an apparently fixed star background, waiting 6 months, and then observing them again.
  • Half of the change in the position of the star relative to the background is known as parallax angle, and is equal to the distance to the star in parsecs
  • The parsec is a measurement of distance directly relating to this measurement system, in which a star of distance = 1 parsec will produce a parallax angle of 1” (arcsecond) when this operation is done
  • As parallax angle increases, the distance to the star decreases, therefore d=1pd = \dfrac{1}{p}d=p1​﻿ and vice versa
  • Where d is defined as the distance to the star in parsec, and p is defined as the parallax angle created in arcsec

Hubble’s Law

  • Hubble, due to his observation that almost everything in the universe is red shifted relative to us, realised that the universe is expanding
  • Things that are further away from us tend to be more red shifted than things closer to us (direct linear correlation) leading Hubble to believe that the expansion of the universe is accelerating linearly
  • Hubble’s law states that the velocity of any object away from us (radial velocity) can be determined using a constant, as well as its distance in megaparsecs

Cepheid Variable Stars

  • These are types of stars that change their relative intensity over specific periods of time, due to various gravitational effects that must not be understood for GCSE
  • The method of calculating the distance to a cepheid variable star is an easy 3 mark question that should be committed to memory
    • Determine the period of the star visually
    • Use the period luminosity law to determine the absolute magnitude of the star using this (calculation not needed)
    • Determine the apparent magnitude of the star visually
    • Use the distance modulus equation to determine the distance to the star
